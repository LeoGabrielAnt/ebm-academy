1. Concept of the Refrigeration Cycle

Foundation of mechanical cooling

The refrigeration cycle is a thermodynamic process used to remove heat from one environment and reject it elsewhere. In direct-expansion (DX) HVAC systems, this heat transfer is performed by a refrigerant fluid that continuously circulates through the main system components.


In practical terms, the refrigerant absorbs heat in the evaporator, is compressed by the compressor, rejects heat in the condenser, and undergoes a pressure reduction in the expansion device before restarting the process. This behavior is the technical basis for virtually every operational analysis in refrigeration.

Important technical note

The system does not “generate cold”; it removes heat from one point and transports it to another. This is one of the most important ideas for an HVAC technician.

2. Thermodynamic Fundamentals

Pressure, temperature, and change of state

Refrigerant behavior depends directly on the relationship between pressure and temperature. When pressure increases, saturation temperature also rises. When pressure decreases, saturation temperature also falls. This relationship is essential for interpreting the cycle.


High pressure

Region of the cycle where the refrigerant flows after compression and before expansion. It is usually associated with compressor discharge and the liquid line.

Low pressure

Region where the refrigerant evaporates and returns to the compressor. It is associated with the evaporator and the suction line.

Evaporation

Process in which the refrigerant absorbs heat and changes from liquid to vapor, mainly in the evaporator.

Condensation

Process in which the refrigerant rejects heat and changes from vapor to liquid, mainly in the condenser.


Any technician who wants to improve diagnostic skills must understand that the refrigerant changes physical state according to heat transfer and the pressure difference within the circuit.

4. Main Components

The technical role of each system element

Compressor

Considered the “heart” of the system. It moves the refrigerant and raises its energy condition.

Condenser

Responsible for heat rejection. Its efficiency depends on cleanliness, air flow, or proper exchange with the condensing medium.

Expansion device

Controls evaporator feeding and creates the pressure reduction required for the evaporation process.

Evaporator

Location where the main heat absorption from the conditioned space occurs. Its performance depends heavily on air flow and coil cleanliness.


Component Main function Common failure symptom
Compressor Raise pressure and move the refrigerant Low capacity, starting failure, overcurrent
Condenser Reject heat High pressure, high discharge temperature
Expansion Control evaporator feeding Low feeding, high SH, instability
Evaporator Absorb heat from the environment Low heat transfer, freezing, low pressure

5. Initial Troubleshooting Reading

How to begin technical reasoning

One of the biggest mistakes in refrigeration is diagnosing a fault based only on pressure. Correct interpretation requires correlation between:

  • suction and discharge pressure;
  • actual line temperatures;
  • superheat and subcooling;
  • air flow through evaporator and condenser;
  • thermal load of the environment;
  • operating history and alarms.

Good practices

Before concluding “low charge” or “compressor failure”, check coil conditions, filters, ventilation, sensors, electrical supply, and operating sequence.


Low pressure

It may be related to low charge, low air flow, restriction, or low thermal load.

High pressure

It may indicate a dirty condenser, poor ventilation, excess refrigerant, or high outdoor temperature.

Liquid return

Critical condition for the compressor. It is normally associated with very low SH and excessive evaporator feeding.

Low capacity

Requires complete analysis of air flow, heat exchangers, refrigeration circuit, setpoints, and actual load condition.

6. Essential Technical Glossary

Core terms in this module

Saturation

Condition in which liquid and vapor coexist in equilibrium at a given pressure.

Superheat

Difference between the actual vapor temperature and the saturation temperature at suction.

Subcooling

Difference between condensing saturation temperature and actual liquid-line temperature.

Delta T

Temperature difference between two points, widely used in air and chilled-water analysis.
